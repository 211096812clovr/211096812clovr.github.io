★ THE MYTH ★

A shortlived synth pop duo formed in Glasgow in 1981 by Jill Bryson and Rose McDowall. Though they never achieved massive commercial success in the West, their signature look - adorning flamboyant polka dots, ribbons and bows - left an indelible mark on the Japanese scene, heavily inspiring the Gothic Lolita fashion movement and bands like Malice Mizer.

★ DOOMED YURI? ★

McDowall and Bryson's self-titled debut album was released in 1985 through Korova Records, a subsidiary of WEA (warner company). According to Rose, they had little control over the production and weren't happy with the finished product, also dealing with a patronizing manager whom they had to fire. Jill was facing severe agoraphobia at the time which prevented them from huge live opportunities and even recording a second album in Japan with Ryuichi Sakamoto. Jill was pushing away from Rose because of her newfound interest in the occult, making neofolk buddies and collecting nazi memorabilia. They broke up in 1986.

★ SHE WAS REALLY SCARED ★


[Jill had] always had agoraphobia as long as I’d known her. It’s a horrible condition to have, when she was panicking she was really scared. It’s not like ‘I don’t want to do this,’ she was really really scared, scared for her life, that kind of fear. And I know what that feels like from a different kind of angle, being scared for your life – I’ve felt that before but not through agoraphobia. Most people were pretty tolerant I think. Her boyfriend was really good cos he’d go places with her.

At the end she was seeing hypnotists and stuff, we’d tried everything, the record company had tried. She’d meditation tapes and all sorts of stuff, and she went to group therapy where people had all sorts of different phobias. And sometimes she would get better. It was completely unpredictable, that was the worst thing about it, it was so completely unpredictable.

★ ONLY ME ★

I Found their music on a board when I was twelve. I was really into J-fashion and BJDs, creepy cute things. Trees and Flowers was a point of contact for me, Jill's lyrics about her agoraphobia made so much sense.

The shift happened later. I stopped wearing the lace and ribbons. Muted colors, quiet clothes. I wanted to be invisible and take up zero space. A surrender to the path of least resistance. It’s easier to go unnoticed than to deal with the constant feedback of being looked at.

Instead of shrinking and conforming, Jill treated her clothes like an armor that protected her from the rest of the world. People are predictable, and the ones who judge are usually bored. It doesn't really matter either way.

✦ POST-FALLOUT ✦

After Strawberry Switchblade, Rose McDowall moved into the neofolk scene, contributing to projects like Psychic TV and Current 93. While the production moved away from 80s synth-pop, the focus on morbidity and isolation remained consistent throughout her later discography.
